Planewave Energy Cut-off and Kpoint Mesh#

After the first tutorial covered the electronic convergence of a DFT calculation, the next step is to get a better understanding of the energy cut-off and kpoint mesh, the two primary convergence parameters of a plane wave DFT simulation code. The electronic smearing \(\sigma\) would be a third parameter and the density of the mesh for sampling the charge desity a fourth one, but these two are not considered at this point.

Following the first tutorial again the SPHInX DFT simulation code is used in combination with the pyiron.org workflow framework. The installation of these software packages is explained in the first tutorial. In analogy to the first tutorial, also the second tutorial starts by importing the Project object in addition to matplotlib and numpy.

Why plane waves?#

In molecular quantum chemistry the Kohn-Sham orbitals are expanded in a finite set of atom-centred basis functions (Gaussians, Slater-type orbitals, …). For an infinitely extended, periodic crystal such as bulk Aluminium this is not convenient, because the same finite molecular basis cannot describe an infinite solid. Instead, plane-wave DFT codes exploit the periodicity of the crystal directly. If the effective potential obeys \(v(\mathbf{r}) = v(\mathbf{r}+\mathbf{R})\) for every lattice vector \(\mathbf{R}\) of the crystal, Bloch’s theorem states that every Kohn-Sham orbital can be written as a lattice-periodic part \(u_{n\mathbf{k}}\) multiplied by a plane wave: $\(\psi_{n\mathbf{k}}(\mathbf{r}) = e^{i\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{r}}\, u_{n\mathbf{k}}(\mathbf{r}), \qquad u_{n\mathbf{k}}(\mathbf{r}+\mathbf{R}) = u_{n\mathbf{k}}(\mathbf{r})\)\( where \)n\( is a band index (analogous to the orbital index in a molecule) and \)\mathbf{k}\( is a crystal momentum restricted to the first Brillouin zone, the reciprocal-space unit cell. Because \)u_{n\mathbf{k}}\( is lattice-periodic it can be expanded exactly in a Fourier series over the reciprocal lattice vectors \)\mathbf{G}\( of the crystal: \)\(u_{n\mathbf{k}}(\mathbf{r}) = \sum_{\mathbf{G}} c_{n,\mathbf{k}+\mathbf{G}}\, e^{i\mathbf{G}\cdot\mathbf{r}} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \psi_{n\mathbf{k}}(\mathbf{r}) = \sum_{\mathbf{G}} c_{n,\mathbf{k}+\mathbf{G}}\, e^{i(\mathbf{k}+\mathbf{G})\cdot\mathbf{r}}\)\( This plane-wave expansion is exact only in the limit of infinitely many \)\mathbf{G}\( vectors. In practice the sum has to be truncated, which is exactly what the planewave energy cut-off does. In addition, any observable that involves a sum over the occupied orbitals of a molecule turns into an integral over the continuous index \)\mathbf{k}\( within the Brillouin zone, which numerically is approximated by a discrete k-point mesh. These two truncations - of \)\mathbf{G}\( and of \)\mathbf{k}$ - are the two convergence parameters studied in this tutorial.

One additional complication of an all-electron plane-wave expansion is that core electron wavefunctions oscillate rapidly close to the nucleus, which would require an impractically large number of plane waves to resolve. SPHInX therefore replaces the core electrons and the singular nuclear Coulomb potential by a smooth pseudopotential (or the closely related projector augmented-wave, PAW, method) that reproduces the scattering properties of the valence electrons while remaining smooth close to the nucleus. Conceptually this plays the same role as a frozen-core approximation in molecular quantum chemistry, but is chosen specifically to keep the plane-wave basis small. This is also why only a moderate number of plane waves, corresponding to a cut-off in the order of a few hundred eV, is typically sufficient to converge total energy differences.

Planewave Energy Cut-off#

While the SPHInX DFT simulation code internally defines the planewave energy cut-off in Hartree pyiron consistently uses electron volt (eV) for the placewave energy cut-off for all DFT simulation codes and also the energies calculated. Beyond eV pyiron uses Angstrom for length, Giga Pascal for pressures and stresses, Kelvin for temperature, femtoseconds for time and atomic mass units for the masses. A full list of the internal units in pyiron is included in the FAQ.

For testing different planewave energy cut-offs a linspace() is created ranging from 100eV to 600eV in a total of 21 steps which results in a step size of 25eV.

Each individual plane wave \(e^{i(\mathbf{k}+\mathbf{G})\cdot\mathbf{r}}\) carries a kinetic energy $\(\varepsilon(\mathbf{k}+\mathbf{G}) = \frac{\hbar^2}{2m_e}\left|\mathbf{k}+\mathbf{G}\right|^2\)\( and the plane-wave energy cut-off \)E_\text{cut}\( simply discards every plane wave whose kinetic energy exceeds this threshold: \)\(\frac{\hbar^2}{2m_e}\left|\mathbf{k}+\mathbf{G}\right|^2 \leq E_\text{cut}\)\( Geometrically this means only the reciprocal lattice vectors \)\mathbf{G}\( inside a sphere of radius \)k_\text{cut} = \sqrt{2m_e E_\text{cut}}/\hbar\( around \)\mathbf{k}\( are kept. Increasing \)E_\text{cut}\( therefore increases the size of the plane-wave basis set, exactly as switching from a smaller to a larger Gaussian basis set (e.g. double-zeta to triple-zeta) increases the size of a molecular basis set. Because the Kohn-Sham total energy is variational, enlarging the basis by increasing \)E_\text{cut}$ can only ever lower (or leave unchanged) the total energy - which is exactly the monotonous convergence observed below.

For the following calculations a planewave energy cut-off of 300eV is chosen, primarily for computational efficiency. In addition, it is important to mention that the planewave energy cut-off is not universal, it has to be tested for any element and even alloys can have different convergence behaviour depending on their electronic structure. So careful convergence tests should be the start of every DFT calculation.

K-point Mesh#

While the planewave energy cut-off limits the number of planewaves to resolve the electronic structure, the k-point mesh represents the sampling in reciprocal space. The finer the k-point mesh the less interpolation is required between the individual k-points to resolve the electronic structure. For the tutorial a kpoint mesh is choosen starting from the Gamma point \(1\times1\times1\) up to a mesh with \(10\times10\times10\) k-points.

In a molecule the electron density is built from a sum over a finite number of occupied molecular orbitals. In a periodic solid the band index \(n\) still runs over a finite (small) number of bands, but for every band \(n\) there is a continuous range of allowed crystal momenta \(\mathbf{k}\) inside the first Brillouin zone, so the sum over occupied states turns into a sum over bands and an integral over the Brillouin zone: $\(n(\mathbf{r}) = \sum_n \int_\text{BZ} f_{n\mathbf{k}}\, \left|\psi_{n\mathbf{k}}(\mathbf{r})\right|^2 \, d\mathbf{k}\)\( This Brillouin-zone integral has no closed form and is instead evaluated numerically on a discrete, regular grid of k-points, most commonly generated with the [Monkhorst-Pack](https://doi.org/10.1103/PhysRevB.13.5188) scheme: \)\(\mathbf{k}_{n_1 n_2 n_3} = \frac{n_1}{N_1}\mathbf{b}_1 + \frac{n_2}{N_2}\mathbf{b}_2 + \frac{n_3}{N_3}\mathbf{b}_3, \qquad n_i = 1, \dots, N_i\)\( where \)\mathbf{b}_1, \mathbf{b}_2, \mathbf{b}_3\( are the reciprocal lattice vectors of the simulation cell. The `set_kpoints([N1, N2, N3])` function used below directly sets the grid dimensions \)N_1\times N_2\times N_3\(. As for any numerical quadrature, a finer grid gives a more accurate approximation to the exact Brillouin zone integral, at the cost of one additional DFT-like calculation per extra k-point (reduced by crystal symmetry, see below). Because Aluminium is a metal with a partially occupied band crossing the Fermi level, resolving its Fermi surface requires a denser k-point mesh (together with electronic smearing \)\sigma\(, mentioned in the introduction) than an insulator or a molecule, which are often adequately described by sampling only the \)\Gamma\(-point, \)\mathbf{k}=0$.

It is important to mention that a \(10\times10\times10\) k-point mesh typically does not result in 1000 k-points as the crystal symmetry is leveraged to choose high symmetry points and reduce the number of k-points required to calculate. Typically the method from Monkhorst and Pack is used to choose high symmetry points.

Again a for-loop is used to iterate over the k-point mesh dimensions and create a SPHInX calculation for each of those. The rest remains the same to compared to the for-loop to analyse the planewave energy cut-off convergence above.

Supercell Size#

The previous supercells were both cubic, so the k-point mesh in reciprocal space was also chosen in a way to have the same number of k-points in each reciprocal dimension. Still not all simulation cells are cubic. For the example of a simulation cell which is doubled in z-direction the number of k-points in this direction can be reduced by a factor 1/2. For illustration the resulting supercell is visualized below.

This can be understood directly from the Monkhorst-Pack formula above: the reciprocal lattice vectors \(\mathbf{b}_i\) and the real-space lattice vectors \(\mathbf{a}_i\) are related by \(\mathbf{a}_i\cdot\mathbf{b}_j = 2\pi\delta_{ij}\), so doubling a real-space lattice vector, e.g. \(\mathbf{a}_3 \rightarrow 2\mathbf{a}_3\), halves the length of the corresponding reciprocal lattice vector, \(\mathbf{b}_3 \rightarrow \mathbf{b}_3/2\). Sampling this shorter reciprocal lattice vector with half as many k-points, \(N_3 \rightarrow N_3/2\), therefore reproduces the same density of k-points per unit of reciprocal space, and hence the same precision, as illustrated below.

structure_Al.repeat([1,1,2]).plot3d()

In analogy to the previous k-point mesh convergence test above the loop iterates over the number of kpoints in one dimension. For this test only the even number of kpoints are considered, so the number of k-points for the z-dimension can be set to be half the number of kpoints in the other dimensions.

energy_tot_kpoint_big_lst = []
for kpoint in kpoint_dim_lst[1::2]:
    job_Al_conv = pr.create.job.Sphinx("spx_Al_kpoint_big_{:.0f}_{:.0f}_{:.0f}".format(kpoint, kpoint, kpoint/2))
    job_Al_conv.structure = structure_Al.repeat([1,1,2])
    job_Al_conv.set_kpoints([kpoint,kpoint,kpoint/2])
    job_Al_conv.set_encut(300.0)
    job_Al_conv.server.cores = 1
    job_Al_conv.run()
    energy_tot_kpoint_big_lst.append(job_Al_conv.output.generic.energy_tot[-1])

As the k-point mesh is defined in reciprocal space, you can achieve the same level of precision by doubling the simulation cell in one direction and setting the k-point mesh to half. Still the additional interaction of the electrons results in this simulation being less efficient, so the computationally most efficient choice for a DFT simulation is choosing the simulation cell as small as possible, but that might restrict the scientific complexity.

Conclusion#

While the accuracy of most DFT calculation is limited by the choice of DFT functional, the precision can also be the dominant source of uncertainty. The precision is primarily related to the planewave energy cut-off and k-point mesh.
